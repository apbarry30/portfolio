Project Overview

For this project, the class was divided into teams of 3-4 students to conduct design thinking and goal directed design. The aim is to propose design solutions to further COURAGE’s work to “identify good practices and increase accountability” regarding domestic violence and sexual assault, both in the U.S. and globally. 2 teams will be assigned to look at the issue at the local/domestic level and 3 teams will be focused at looking at the issue from a global perspective to understand how the needs compare and contrast. For this assignment, my team of 3 took a global perspective, focusing on Latin America.

For this course assignment, we will employ human centered design (HCD) techniques both in-class and outside of class to identify target stakeholders and users and their needs. A team member from COURAGE specializing on this topic will be available to us. This includes but is not limited to stakeholder mapping, interviewing, and competitive analysis of things they are currently using.

1. Purpose

Assuming the role of designers, we will work in small teams alongside a law student who will be our subject matter expert and key COURAGE (Community Oriented and United Responses to Address Gender Violence and Equality) stakeholder to become familiar with the complex subject matter of domestic violence and sexual assault. Through this collaboration and design process, the purpose of the project is to propose the design of information systems and other interactive solutions that may assist in identifying and preventing gender bias in law enforcement responses to domestic violence and sexual assault.

2. Objective

For law enforcement, advocates, victims/survivors of domestic violence and sexual assault, or any other group of people who are looking for help, guidance, information or statistics about this topic, our public portal web will provide them with a simple and powerful tool to approach their needs. Different from the current COURAGE web page, our portal web combines the aesthetics with a clean and organized way to show the information to be easily founded by any person who is looking for assistance in identifying, preventing or who is going through a problem of domestic violence and sexual assault. Furthermore, the intention of this solution is to reach countries and regions from US and Latin America, thus it will provide the option to see the information in different languages as well as printable resources to give information to communities that don’t have easy access to technology.

    The main goals for this project are:

  • Offer an alternative to the current information from the COURAGE web page and organize and adapt it to be easily understandable by any person.
  • Assist law enforcement, advocates and victims/survivor of domestic violence and sexual assault by having three specific sections to find information concerned to what they need, it will help them to save time and feel comfortable with any situation they may be facing.
  • Offer an accessible solution for people in different regions and countries in US and Latin America.
  • Include a victim centered approach by offering resources as hotlines, police stations, physicians or advocates information according to the location of the victim.

Home Truth

My first impression about this topic came for Home Truth, a documentary film detailing a domestic violence survivor’s groundbreaking quest for justice that reached the U.S. Supreme Court and an international human rights tribunal. I attended the film screening at University of Miami and also joined a panel discussion featuring the filmmakers, human rights experts, and the survivor-activist herself – Jessica Lenahan.

Home Truth available here tells the story of Jessica Gonzales, who had experienced every parent’s worst nightmare when her three young daughters were killed after being abducted by their father in violation of a restraining order. Devastated, Jessica filed a lawsuit against the police, claiming they did not adequately enforce her restraining order despite her repeated calls for help that night. Determined to make sure her daughters did not die in vain, Jessica has pursued her case all the way to the U.S. Supreme Court and an international human rights tribunal, seeking to strengthen legal rights for domestic violence victims.

Rosie Hidalgo:

Senior Director of Public Policy for Casa de Esperanza: National Latin@ Network for Healthy Families and Communities Rosie previously served as the Deputy Director for Policy at the Office on Violence Against Women (OVW) at the U.S. Department of Justice. Prior to working at OVW, Rosie had worked as the National Policy Director at Casa/NLN and served on the Steering Committee of the National Task Force to End Sexual and Domestic Violence where she played an active role in national advocacy efforts to reauthorize the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA) in 2013. Rosie previously worked at the National Latino Alliance for the Elimination of Domestic Violence and as an attorney at legal services programs for low-income families in New York City and in Northern Virginia. Rosie, the daughter of immigrants from Cuba, has spent a total of ten years as an adult living in three different countries in Latin America, including living in the Dominican Republic from 2002-2006, where she helped establish and coordinate a community-based domestic violence prevention and intervention network and worked as a consultant for the World Bank on social services reforms

Fortunately, we had the chance to talk with Rosie. Several key takeaways from our talk with Rosie: When taking cases about domestic violence, it’s important to respect victims if they don’t want to call and report the case, a common phrase is “the police make it worst”. Furthermore, in domestic violence cases, sometimes jail is not the solution because it could result in economic instability or revenge with contacts in high positions. She also talked a lot about data: The number of calls to report domestic violence was high, and law enforcement officials did not put enough resources to answer them. However, then the number of calls decreased, but the number of opened cases without solution increased. She thinks that a possible solution is a system to sort and display data to easily analyze and find the best approach to address the problems. She also said that we could use data to monitor what law enforcement is doing, or even a simple mobile app that could help people to report violence. Videos could also achieve this, for example “tea consent” See video here is a video that teach about consent making the analogy with a cup of tea.

Fernando Bertoncello:

Brazilian lawyer and current student at UM’s School of Law’s COURAGE A big case that we learned from Fernando was Maria Da Penha, a case that reached the Inter American Commission of Human Rights (similar to Jessica Lenahan case). The case da Penha filed languished in court for two decades, while Maria's husband remained free. Years later, in a landmark ruling, the Court of Human Rights criticized the Brazilian government for not taking effective measures to prosecute and convict perpetrators of domestic violence. In response to this, the Brazilian government in 2006 enacted a law under the symbolic name “Maria da Penha Law on Domestic and Family Violence. Something else that Fernando emphasized that even though laws change, culture doesn’t change; Brazil has some very forward-thinking laws but they aren’t generally accepted in society.

To address this situation, women’s groups in the region have begun to promote special women’s police units to be run and staffed by women. Brazil was the first country to establish these special units in 1985 with five countries following suit. These centers are a example of victim centered solutions which should be replicated in many other countries.

Fernando also told us about the necessity of women to be represented by women; however he also mentioned intersectionality, which is a widespread problem that it's worth paying attention.

Intersectionality, "The interconnected nature of social categorizations such as race, class, and gender, regarded as creating overlapping and interdependent systems of discrimination or disadvantage; a theoretical approach based on such a premise." (Oxford Dictionary)

Design Precedents

Below is a brief summary of our design precedents. From social campaigns to virtual reality designs, there are solutions aimed at assisting victims of domestic violence and sexual assault. However there is no one place where you can find compiled information about them. Three of the web pages listed will prove to be useful for creating a solution: the COURAGE web page, University of Miami It’s On Us web page, and the World Health Organization home page. The COURAGE page contains a lot of really good information; however, it is not well organized which makes it hard to understand and read. Furthermore we liked the It’s On Us web page because it has a clear organization and many options for students who are seeking help. Finally, the web page for World Health Organization contains a language translation tool as well as an option to filter information by regions, which makes it more helpful and approachable. We believe that a combination of these 3 pages would be a great solution to achieve our purpose.

Requirements

Requirement Definition 1 The solution shall address, in a sustained manner, a way to arrange existing information and organize it to help identifying and preventing gender bias in law enforcement responses to domestic violence and sexual assault.
2 The solution shall use the current information form COURAGE web page.
3 The solution shall be inclusive to address domestic violence and sexual assault in the U.S. and Latin America.
4 The solution shall show the information organized in three categories related to: Law enforcement, Advocates, and Victims and survivors.
5 Aside the three categories mentioned in Requirement 4, the solution shall contain information to be easily founded by any person who doesn’t consider himself or herself belong to any of those three categories.
6 The solution shall have the option to change the language. Initially it will have the option to change from English to Spanish; in the future it could include other languages as Portuguese. 7 The solution shall have an administrator that keeps information updated and translated to all the offered languages.
8 The solution shall offer printable resources with relevant information in order to reach communities that lack of technology.
9 The solution shall show, in all sections, a hotline or an optional resource to report domestic violence or sexual assault.
10 The solution shall maintain a clean and simple aesthetics.
11 The solution shall address the problem of domestic violence and sexual assault carefully and use appropriate language.
12 The solution shall be accessible from any web browser as Google Chrome, Internet Explorer, Firefox or Safari.
13 The solution shall include the information of COURAGE project and current work.
14 The solution shall include relevant upcoming events that may help to assist in the problem of domestic violence and sexual assault.
15 The solution shall include the DOJ guidance for law enforcement.

Key Functionality

The key factors driving our design solution included accessibility and clear information. After completing an audit of design precedents, we felt that a stakeholder visiting the site should be able to quickly access the information they need depending on who they are. For example, a victim, law enforcement or advocate may be looking for information on how to deal with a domestic violence situation and they would all need different resources to get the right information. We found that there was an overwhelming amount of information without a clear way of arriving to the information that you may need. For the user to find what they need efficiently, we decided to make an easy portal to enter the information once they identify who they are (victim/survivor, law enforcement or advocate). In addition, all the information must be available in multiple languages spoken around the world, as well as be up to date/relevant according to your location. To solve this issue, we decided to add a GPS option in order for a victim to know their rights in their area as well as where they can go for help. In addition, if a person quickly wants information without identifying themselves through the portal, we added quick links to important information on the header for all pages. Users should be able to download information as PDF printable in order to easily have this information handy and share it within their community. This website will help victims around the world to get help, law enforcement to follow correct procedures, and advocates to spread awareness on domestic violence.
